Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution:

TideRider Water Quality Robot

Mechanical design of TideRider

Overview

During summers 2019 - 2023, I was the primary engineer developing TideRider, a low-cost underwater robot for coastal water quality monitoring. I brought the project from an early stage prototype to a field proven design. In summer of 2022 I conducted 4 ocean deployments, the longest of which lasted 10 days.

TideRider is still being used today. Based on my work, the project received funding for 2024 and 2025 to use TideRider to collect dissolved oxygen data in local harbors.

Skills: Solidworks, C++, C, Javascript, Python, PCB assembly and debugging, oscilloscope, autonomy, field operations

TideRider operational model

Design

TideRider uses a novel low-cost buoyancy engine. The engine is passive and bi-stable: it works by leveraging water pressure to collapse a bladder. At the surface the bladder is fully expanded. A propeller will force the vehicle to dive, increasing the ambient water pressure and collapsing the bladder. A few meters below the surface the bladder will have collapsed enough to allow the vehicle to sink without the thruster, and drift to the bottom. The propeller is then used in reverse to bring the vehicles back to the surface and restore the bladder. I implemented this concept by categorizing various bladder types, eventually landing on using a COTs piston and cylinder to act as a virtual bladder.

TideRider can be built from off-the-shelf components and hand tools. It uses cellular connectivity to allow for real time communication on the surface. I designed the vehicle, wrote its control system, and integrated its electrical components.

Field Trials and Navigation 

TideRider is a profiler: it can only actively control its depth. TideRider navigates passively by leveraging favorable tides; it drifts on the surface when the tide is pushing it in a desired direction, and it anchors to the bottom when the tide is unfavorable. 

I was able to prove this behavior in field trials, where TiderRider showed its capability to navigate both along and across the dominant tidal direction. During the longest deployment, the device completed a 6 mile loop over 10 days, returning to its starting location for recovery.

TideRider Control Website

TideRider Control is a website I created that displays data from the device in real time and provides an interface to configure the vehicle. TideRider is actively being used, so the website may show different deployments or perhaps be broken if the team is testing new software. It can take a bit to load. Check it out below! Direct link: https://tide-rider.github.io/.

You can select which device to view by choosing the device number at the top. After it loads, you can scroll down to see the track it followed on the map. The logs may or may not include ocean deployments depending on recent testing.
